Is Prompt Engineering a Competency?

Author: Axiom Methodology Team · June 2026

Recently, "Prompt Engineering" (the skill of writing commands for AI models) has become one of the most popular titles in corporate training catalogs. Everyone is trying to create AI experts by teaching employees how to write prompts with the right words. But is knowing how to write prompts a true business competency?

The core thesis we advocate at Axiom is this: Knowing AI tools is not a competency. Tools change, update, and simplify rapidly; the real competency lies in knowing what to produce with that tool.

1. The Line Between Tool Knowledge and Competency

If you teach an employee how to write formulas in Excel, you teach them a tool. However, if you want them to build a meaningful financial analysis or budget model in that spreadsheet, what they need is financial competency.

The same goes for AI. Knowing how to input "write me an email draft" into ChatGPT or Claude is just tool knowledge (prompt writing). However, making that email persuasive, incorporating the correct sales strategy, and addressing client objections requires deep sales competency.

"AI cannot give you answers unless you know what to ask. The ability to ask the right question is a matter of professional maturity and competency, not tool syntax."

2. The Answer of the E = Y × AI² Formula

The **E = Y × AI²** formula at the heart of our methodology answers this question mathematically. Where:

  • **Y (Competency):** Problem-solving, critical thinking, domain experience, and communication maturity.
  • **AI (Artificial Intelligence):** Prompt writing, tool integration, and automation skills.

If an employee's professional competency (Y) is weak, being able to write excellent prompts (high AI) will not save the impact (E) of their work. AI will turn a mediocre question into a mediocre answer in seconds. However, when a high-competency specialist uses AI as a multiplier, outstanding business results emerge.

3. What is Real Competency?

What will be sought after in the future is not "prompt engineers," but competent professionals who can use AI as a multiplier in their own fields of expertise. For this reason, corporate training budgets should focus not only on teaching AI tools, but on developing core human competencies—such as leadership, sales, and customer experience—in an integrated manner with AI.
